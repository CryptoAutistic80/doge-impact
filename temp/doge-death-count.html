Methodology

Unlike the domestic categories (which lack formal mortality models), the global health death toll from DOGE's USAID dismantlement is backed by peer-reviewed academic research. The primary counter uses the methodology of Prof. Brooke Nichols (Boston University School of Public Health), whose Impact Counter model was independently peer-reviewed and published in the Journal of the International AIDS Society.

Nichols's model estimated 781,343 excess deaths by January 24, 2026 — the one-year anniversary of the funding freeze — at which point the counter was retired. The rate at that point was approximately 88 deaths per hour, or 2,112 per day.

This page extrapolates forward from that baseline at the established daily rate, as the funding cuts remain in effect and have not been reversed. If anything, this is conservative: the Center for Global Development's December 2025 update estimated 500,000–1,000,000 deaths from FY2025 cuts alone, and a February 2026 Lancet study projects 9.4 million excess deaths globally from combined aid cuts by 2030.

The "unquantified" domestic categories (VA, HHS, CDC, NIH, Medicare) are listed without numerical estimates because no peer-reviewed mortality model yet exists for those impacts. They are included to show the full scope of DOGE's actions. As formal studies emerge, they can be incorporated.

Elon Musk's personal responsibility is attributed based on his documented role as de facto leader of DOGE (as found by a federal judge), his personal direction of the USAID dismantlement, and his public dismissal of the death toll ("zero people have died"). Senator Schatz cited 360,000 deaths on the Senate floor. Harvard's Atul Gawande cited 600,000 in The New Yorker. The Lancet published peer-reviewed estimates of millions more to come.
